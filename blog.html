A Glimpse Into the Future of AI Electric Cars

Exploring the rapid advancements in AI, EVs, and the auto industry.

Posted on October 31, 2024

Introduction

Last week, I took part in a radio show discussing AI and electric cars of the future. Based on an article from BBC’s TopGear (my old favorite car show), this got me thinking about how fast things are moving. There’s a lot of misinformation out there on when autonomous driving will be viable, the current state of electric vehicles, and whether hydrogen could replace batteries. I’m particularly interested in how China is advancing with EV technology, despite quality control issues.

Electric Car Status

The pressure on electric car sales is rising as demand slows due to continued quality issues — especially with Tesla’s Cybertruck — political controversies, and marketing gaps. Tesla’s struggles impact the whole industry, given its leadership in the EV market. Despite the promise of fewer mechanical issues, EVs have been surprisingly prone to malfunctions, with repair rates much higher than internal combustion engine (ICE) vehicles. Surveys suggest over half of EV owners might go back to ICE cars.

Some automakers are pivoting to plug-in hybrid electric vehicles (PHEVs) instead of going fully electric. However, PHEVs face challenges: two powertrains mean higher complexity. Additionally, gas engines in PHEVs need regular usage to avoid issues like gummed-up injectors. Pro tip: if you drive a PHEV, look for ethanol-free gas to reduce maintenance problems.

Switch to Tesla's Charging Standard

The U.S. and Europe are moving to Tesla’s plug standard — now known as the North American Charging Standard (NACS) — which should simplify the charging process by 2026. Current EV owners can use adapters but should wait for the software update enabling Supercharger compatibility. Tesla’s system is simpler: just plug in, unlike the old U.S. standard, which required an app activation and sometimes didn’t work due to broken chargers.

Battery Technology

We’ve made massive progress in battery tech. By 2027, new battery types, including solid-state and sodium-ion batteries, promise up to 750-mile ranges and quick charging under 10 minutes. China’s XPeng already offers a 500-mile range car at competitive prices, threatening legacy carmakers. These advances mean EVs will increasingly rival gas-powered cars.

AI and Autonomous Cars

Autonomous cars are currently around Level 2+ (basic obstacle avoidance), with Level 5 (fully autonomous) expected around 2030. Level 3 is debuting in select U.S. regions with Mercedes. Meanwhile, in-car AI digital assistants are becoming more advanced. Nvidia has developed systems enabling drivers to interact with their cars in natural language, from parking requests to locating restaurants.

AI will soon help diagnose car issues in real time, replacing vague check-engine lights with clear, actionable guidance. Over time, your car’s assistant could adjust settings based on your habits and schedule, from news updates on the way to work to entertainment on the drive home.

Wrapping Up

I expect EV and AI car technology to mature by 2030, especially in high-end models. Soon, autonomous cars will allow us to enjoy journeys more safely. AI-driven vehicles, which don’t get distracted, will greatly reduce accidents. Future generations may not even feel the need to own cars, opting for autonomous car subscriptions instead. As a car enthusiast, I look forward to the balance of technology and driving pleasure that lies ahead.
